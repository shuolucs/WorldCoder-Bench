🌑 Understanding Solar Eclipses

A solar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes between the Sun and Earth, casting a shadow on Earth's surface. Despite the Sun being about 400 times larger than the Moon, it is also roughly 400 times farther away, making them appear nearly the same size in our sky.

During a total solar eclipse, the Moon completely covers the Sun's disk, revealing the Sun's corona — its outer atmosphere. This dramatic event can cause temporary darkness, temperature drops, and behavioral changes in animals.

Neurological Response: Research shows that solar eclipses trigger measurable responses in the human nervous system. The sudden darkness activates the brain's suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), which regulates circadian rhythms. The amygdala may also respond to the unusual environmental change, producing feelings of awe or unease. The brainstem model in this simulation represents these neurological connections.

In this simulation, watch as the Moon orbits and periodically aligns between Earth and the Sun. Notice how the lighting changes dramatically during eclipse events!
